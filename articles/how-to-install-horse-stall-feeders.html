How to Install Horse Stall Feeders: A Step-by-Step Guide for Barn Safety

Installing a new feeder in your horse’s stall is more than just a quick hardware project; it is a critical task that directly impacts your horse’s safety, digestive health, and daily routine. Whether you are upgrading from an old, rusted trough or outfitting a brand-new barn, mounting your equipment correctly ensures it stays put when your horse is nudging it, rubbing against it, or enjoying their evening hay.

If you are wondering how to install horse stall feeders the right way, you have come to the right place. In this guide, we will walk you through the preparation, the tools you need, and the essential safety precautions to keep your equine partner happy and secure.

Why Proper Installation Matters

Horses are powerful, curious, and sometimes destructive animals. A feeder that isn’t properly anchored can become a safety hazard, leading to sharp edges, injury, or the feeder being ripped off the wall entirely during a midnight "stall tantrum."

By learning how to install horse stall feeders with the correct mounting hardware and at the right height, you minimize the risk of injury and ensure that the feeder can withstand the wear and tear of daily use.

Tools and Materials Needed

Before you pick up a drill, gather these supplies to ensure the job goes smoothly:

  • The Feeder: Choose between corner feeders, wall-mounted troughs, or hay racks depending on your horse’s habits.
  • Heavy-Duty Drill: Cordless drills are fine, but ensure the battery is fully charged.
  • Appropriate Fasteners: Use lag bolts, carriage bolts, or heavy-duty mounting screws. Avoid basic wood screws, as they lack the shear strength required for equine use.
  • Washers: Large fender washers are vital to distribute the pressure and prevent the bolt from pulling through the plastic or metal of the feeder.
  • Level: A crooked feeder isn't just an eyesore; it can lead to uneven wear and wasted grain.
  • Tape Measure: For consistent placement.
  • Stud Finder: Essential for finding structural support in the stall wall.

Step-by-Step: How to Install Horse Stall Feeders

Follow these steps to ensure a secure and durable installation.

1. Choose the Optimal Height

The height of your feeder is crucial for your horse's comfort. As a general rule, the top of the feeder should be at approximately chest height. If it is too high, the horse may drop food; too low, and it increases the risk of the horse stepping into the feeder or getting a hoof caught.

Consult the manufacturer’s recommendations for your specific model, as some corner feeders have very specific mounting requirements.

2. Locate Structural Supports

Never rely on thin stall boards or plywood alone to hold a feeder. You need to anchor your hardware into a structural post or a horizontal framing member. Use a stud finder or look for existing screws in the stall walls to identify where the heavy-duty framing is located.

3. Position and Mark

Hold the feeder up to the wall at your desired height. Use a level to ensure it is perfectly horizontal. Once aligned, take a pencil or a permanent marker and mark the holes on the wall through the mounting brackets of the feeder.

4. Pre-Drill Pilot Holes

This is the most skipped step, but it is the most important. Pre-drilling prevents the wood from splitting, which is a common issue in older barns. Use a drill bit that is slightly smaller than the shank of your lag bolts or screws. This creates a clean path for the fastener and allows for a "tight bite" into the wood.

5. Secure the Feeder

Position the feeder back over your pilot holes. Insert your bolts with the fender washers already in place. Start by tightening each bolt until it is "snug" rather than fully tight. Once all bolts are in place, go back and tighten them fully. Ensure the feeder is flush against the wall so there are no gaps where a horse’s halter or lip could get caught.

Tips for a Professional Finish

  • Check for Sharp Ends: Ensure that the tips of your bolts do not protrude through the other side of the wall. If they do, use an angle grinder to cut them flush or cover them with a protective bolt cap to prevent injury to a horse in the adjacent stall.
  • Smooth Edges: If you are using a plastic feeder, inspect it for any burrs or sharp factory edges. Sand these down so they don’t irritate the horse's muzzle.
  • Accessibility: Ensure the feeder is positioned in a way that allows you to dump grain or hay easily from the hallway or stall door without having to enter the "strike zone" of a pushy horse.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the best height to mount a corner feeder?

For most average-sized horses, the top rim of the feeder should sit roughly 36 to 42 inches from the floor. Always observe your horse eating; if they are straining their neck downward or tossing their head, the height may need adjustment.

Can I install a feeder on a metal stall wall?

Yes, but you will need different hardware. Instead of lag screws, you will need to drill through the metal and use carriage bolts with locking nuts on the opposite side. Ensure the bolt heads are smooth (carriage bolts) so the horse does not rub against a sharp edge.

How often should I check the feeder mounts?

You should perform a "safety check" at least once a month. Over time, the vibration and weight of a horse nudging the feeder can cause hardware to loosen. Check for wobbly bolts and ensure the feeder is still firmly flush against the wall.

Should I use washers when installing feeders?

Absolutely. Using large fender washers prevents the fastener from pulling through the feeder’s plastic or metal mounting hole, especially if the horse happens to bump into the unit with force. It significantly increases the longevity of your installation.
